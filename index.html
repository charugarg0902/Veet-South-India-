→
Phase 1 cultural pushback was real but minority
~19% of Phase 1 South India reviews flagged cultural discomfort with the Bikini Ad. Concentrated in Tamil Nadu and Telangana, primarily on Twitter/X. The majority — 52% — responded positively to the empowerment angle.
→
Myth Busting & Other Ads felt more culturally neutral
Phase 2 and 3 saw near-zero "culturally inappropriate" signals. Product-functional ads (wax strips, sensitive skin, razor comparison) bypassed the cultural sensitivity entirely — though Phase 2's weakest overall sentiment suggests digital-only reach was the drag, not content.
→
Kerala is culturally aligned — a template market
Kerala's 70.6% positive and absence of "culturally inappropriate" signals suggests the bold, empowerment-led positioning works well here. Could be used as a test market for bolder future campaigns before broader South India rollout.

!
Skin darkening is a real and active concern — not a fringe signal
"Dark patches" and "hyperpigmentation" together account for approximately 38–66% of the negative reviews in South India. This is not a small edge case — it is the dominant negative theme in southern markets and likely suppresses trial among first-time buyers.
!
Concern is about long-term use, not single application
Review language suggests the skin darkening perception builds over repeated use — "over time," "after a few months." This is harder to counter with a single campaign and may require a targeted ingredient communication or a dermatologist partnership in South India specifically.
→
Myth Busting & Other Ads did not address this directly
Neither Phase 2 nor Phase 3 ads appear to have tackled the skin darkening belief head-on. The positive sentiment recovery in Phase 3 was driven by product efficacy and wax strip messaging — not by addressing this specific South India concern. It remains an open gap.
